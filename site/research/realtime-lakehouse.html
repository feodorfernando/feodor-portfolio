Real-Time Lakehouse Architectures: Streaming, CDC & the Table-Format Wars

Open table formats turned the data lake into a transactional, real-time store. This is what they enable, how Iceberg / Delta / Hudi differ, and the operational realities of running streaming tables in production.

Feodor Fernando• Lakehouse Research• Rev. 2026-06

1. Why "lakehouse" exists

The lake gave us cheap, open storage but no transactions; the warehouse gave us ACID and performance but locked data in a proprietary engine. The lakehouse merges them: an open table format adds ACID commits, snapshot isolation, schema evolution, and row-level updates on top of object storage — so one copy of data serves BI, ML, and streaming, readable by many engines.

2. What makes a lakehouse "real-time"

  • Streaming ingestion — micro-batch commits (Spark/Flink) land data seconds-to-minutes after it's produced, each batch an atomic snapshot.
  • Change Data Capture (CDC) — database changes (Debezium) replayed as row-level upserts/deletes, keeping a queryable mirror of operational state.
  • Row-level mutations — merge-on-read deletes make updates/deletes cheap enough to apply continuously.
  • Snapshot isolation — readers see a consistent table while writers stream into it.

Iceberg's differentiators: hidden partitioning (queries don't reference partition columns, and you can evolve the spec without rewriting history) and the broadest cross-engine reach via the REST catalog. Delta is strongest inside the Databricks ecosystem; Hudi was purpose-built for high-frequency upserts.

4. Copy-on-write vs merge-on-read

The decision that defines your latency/cost profile, common to all three formats:

              write cost   read cost              best for
  COW         high         low                    append-mostly, batch
  MOR         low          grows with deletes     streaming upserts / CDC

Rule of thumb for streaming: ingest with MOR, then compact aggressively to fold delete files back in and keep read amplification bounded.

5. The operational realities (where projects fail)

  • Small files — streaming commits create millions of tiny files; compaction (rewrite_data_files) is non-negotiable.
  • Delete-file accumulation — under MOR, every update adds a delete file; monitor the delete ratio and rewrite position deletes.
  • Snapshot/metadata growth — expire snapshots and rewrite manifests to keep planning fast.
  • Orphan files — failed commits leave unreferenced files; clean them.
  • Exactly-once — achieved via checkpoints + idempotent MERGE, not by trying to force it across the whole chain.

This is exactly the shape of the companion Iceberg Streaming Lakehouse and CDC → Iceberg projects.

7. Choosing a format in 2026

  • Vendor-neutral, multi-engine, long horizon → Iceberg.
  • Databricks-centric shop → Delta (deepest native integration).
  • Extremely high-frequency upserts → Hudi is worth evaluating.
  • Convergence is happening (UniForm / cross-format reads) — bet on the open catalog layer, not a single engine.

8. Takeaways

  • Open table formats make the lake transactional and real-time.
  • MOR for streaming, COW for batch; compaction is the price of real-time.
  • The hard part is operations, not ingestion — budget for maintenance from day one.
